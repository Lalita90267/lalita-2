Art Styles Overview

1. Realism

Realism emerged in the 19th century as an attempt to represent subjects accurately without idealization. Artists focused on portraying everyday life, often highlighting ordinary people and scenes in a true-to-life manner.

2. Cubism

Pioneered by Picasso and Braque, Cubism breaks objects into geometric shapes, representing multiple perspectives at once. This early 20th-century style emphasizes abstract forms over realistic detail.

3. Surrealism

Surrealism explores the subconscious mind, often featuring dreamlike or bizarre imagery. Artists like Salvador Dalí created works that merge fantasy with reality, often with unusual juxtapositions and symbolic elements.

4. Abstract Art

Abstract art rejects realistic depiction, focusing on colors, shapes, and forms to convey meaning. Pioneered by artists like Wassily Kandinsky, this style allows for open interpretation and emphasizes visual impact over subject matter.

5. Pop Art

Developed in the 1950s and '60s, Pop Art celebrates popular culture and consumerism, using bold colors and familiar imagery. Artists like Andy Warhol turned everyday objects and celebrities into iconic art pieces.

Featured Artists

Leonardo da Vinci (1452–1519)

Known for masterpieces like *Mona Lisa* and *The Last Supper*, Leonardo da Vinci was a Renaissance artist, scientist, and inventor. His detailed studies of human anatomy and groundbreaking techniques have had a lasting impact on Western art.


Vincent van Gogh (1853–1890)

A post-impressionist painter, Van Gogh created iconic works such as *Starry Night* and *Sunflowers*. Known for his expressive brushwork and vivid color, his emotional and personal style is celebrated worldwide.


Pablo Picasso (1881–1973)

One of the most influential artists of the 20th century, Picasso co-founded the Cubist movement and experimented with various styles. His famous works include *Les Demoiselles d'Avignon* and *Guernica*, which reflect his bold approach to form and perspective.


Frida Kahlo (1907–1954)

Mexican painter Frida Kahlo is known for her self-portraits and vivid explorations of identity, pain, and culture. Her unique style blends realism with surreal elements, creating an emotional and symbolic body of work.

Art History

1. Prehistoric Art (30,000–2,000 BCE)

Prehistoric art includes cave paintings, carvings, and sculptures created by early humans. These works, such as those in the Lascaux Caves, are characterized by depictions of animals and daily life, and are some of the earliest forms of visual expression.

2. Ancient Art (3,000 BCE–400 CE)

Ancient art spans the Egyptian, Greek, Roman, and Mesopotamian civilizations, with art that often focused on religious, mythological, and cultural themes. Highlights include Egyptian hieroglyphs, Greek sculptures like the Parthenon Marbles, and Roman mosaics.

3. Medieval Art (400–1400)

Medieval art was largely religious, featuring illuminated manuscripts, Gothic architecture, and Byzantine mosaics. This period included significant artistic developments such as the use of stained glass in cathedrals and religious icons in Eastern Europe.

DIY Art Projects

Few projects to try:

1. Abstract Pour Painting

Create beautiful, fluid abstract art using the paint pouring technique.

Materials:
  • Acrylic paints in various colors
  • Pouring medium
  • Canvas or thick paper
  • Cups and stirring sticks
Steps:
  1. Mix each color of paint with pouring medium in separate cups.
  2. Layer the colors in one cup, then pour onto the canvas.
  3. Tilt the canvas to let the colors flow and create patterns.
  4. Let dry for 24 hours, then admire your abstract masterpiece!

2. Watercolor Resist Art

Use wax or oil pastels to create patterns that "resist" watercolor, revealing fun designs.

Materials:
  • Watercolor paints
  • White paper or watercolor paper
  • Brushes and water
Steps:
  1. Draw a pattern or design on the paper with the white crayon.
  2. Paint over the design with watercolors, and watch the crayon resist the paint.
  3. Experiment with different color combinations for a unique effect.

3. Pressed Flower Art

Make a natural art piece using pressed flowers to create delicate compositions.

Materials:
  • Flowers or leaves
  • Heavy books (for pressing)
  • Glue
Steps:
  1. Place flowers between pages of a heavy book and leave for a week to press.
  2. Arrange the pressed flowers on paper and glue them in place.
  3. Frame your finished piece or use it as a nature-inspired greeting card.

Art Supplies Recommendations

1. Paints

Explore quality options for different painting mediums like acrylics, watercolors, and oils.

Recommended Brands:
  • Acrylics: Golden, Liquitex, Winsor & Newton
  • Watercolors: Daniel Smith, M. Graham, Sennelier
  • Oils: Gamblin, Old Holland, Michael Harding

2. Brushes

The right brushes can make a big difference in your artwork. Each type serves a different purpose in different mediums.

Recommended Types:
  • Round Brushes: Great for detail work and fine lines.
  • Flat Brushes: Ideal for bold strokes and filling larger areas.
  • Filbert Brushes: Excellent for blending and creating soft edges.

3. Sketching Pencils

Pencils come in different hardness levels, affecting the quality and depth of your sketches.

Recommended Brands: Staedtler, Faber-Castell, Derwent
  • 2H-6H: Light, hard pencils for fine details
  • HB-2B: General-purpose sketching
  • 4B-8B: Soft pencils for shading and bold lines
